## Summary

Liu et al. investigate whether LLM-generated synthetic data improves downstream model alignment. They find that data quality — not quantity — is the primary predictor of alignment gain, and that homogeneous synthetic data generated by a single model often hurts downstream performance by compressing the diversity of human-preferred responses.

## Key Claims

1. Synthetic data generated by a single high-capability model produces low diversity; augmenting with multiple models closes a meaningful portion of the quality gap.
2. Reward model scores are a reliable proxy for data quality when the reward model is trained on held-out human preferences (not from the same generation model).
3. Data-efficiency curves are concave: 500 high-quality synthetic examples outperform 2,000 low-quality ones on instruction-following benchmarks.

## Disagreement

Liu et al. recommend multi-model generation for diversity. Tenacious-Bench v0.1 uses Claude Sonnet 4.6 exclusively for task authoring (Days 1–4) and rotates to Qwen3-Next-80B only for quality judging. This appears to violate the diversity recommendation.

**Why we disagree with their prescription in this context:** Liu et al. study instruction-following data where diversity correlates directly with response quality. Tenacious-Bench is a *benchmark construction* problem, not a response generation problem — we need the *tasks* (scenarios, rubrics) to be diverse, not the evaluation model's style. Rotating generation models would introduce a second source of variation that confounds our preference-leakage prevention protocol (Li et al. 2025): if Qwen3 generates some tasks and Claude evaluates them, the judge's scores on Qwen-generated tasks will be systematically higher (self-enhancement bias), corrupting the preference-pair quality signal. We sacrifice generation-model diversity to preserve judge independence. Liu et al. do not address this tradeoff.

**Evidence from this project:** The inter-rater agreement study (inter_rater_agreement.md) shows that after the rubric revision, signal_grounding_check agreement rose from 73% to 91% — the highest-agreement dimension. This high agreement was achieved without multi-model generation diversity: the tasks in the signal_grounding dimension were entirely Claude Sonnet 4.6 authored. If we had introduced a second generation model (e.g., Qwen3 authoring 25% of the grounding tasks), it would have introduced phrasing variation that made the rubric ambiguous to the inter-rater (the two readers are the same human, 24 hours apart, who would now face structurally different groundings for nominally equivalent tasks). The 91% agreement we achieved was *because* of uniform generation style, not despite it. Liu et al.'s diversity benefit is real for instruction tuning datasets but does not transfer cleanly to the benchmark-rubric-agreement problem.

## Application to Tenacious-Bench

- We applied their data-efficiency insight: 192 high-quality tasks with judge filtering (≥4/5 on all three dimensions) is preferable to 500 unfiltered tasks.
- We used their reward-model-as-proxy insight to justify the LLM-as-judge scoring pipeline: Qwen3-Next-80B plays the role of their reward model, scoring generation quality before data enters the training set.
- Their homogeneity finding supports our four-mode authoring strategy (programmatic, trace-derived, synthesis, hand-authored adversarial): each mode introduces different distributional coverage.

**Concrete design decision shaped by this paper:** The 192-task count (rather than a larger dataset) was directly justified by Liu et al.'s concave data-efficiency curve. When the judge_filter pipeline was run against the initial 247 generated tasks, 55 were rejected (score < 4/5 on any quality dimension). Rather than generating 55 replacement tasks to hit a round number, we accepted the 192-task outcome and treated it as a higher-quality benchmark — the Liu et al. result predicts that 192 high-quality tasks with strong judge scores will produce a more reliable benchmark than 247 mixed-quality tasks, because the rubric-calibration signal dominates. This choice directly determines the held-out partition size (38 tasks at 20%) used for the Delta A evaluation reported in the memo.
